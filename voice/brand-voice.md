## Voice Attributes

**Direct**: get to the point. No filler paragraphs, no warming up to the topic. The first sentence should already be useful.

**Data-driven**: back claims with numbers. "CPL dropped 34% over 6 weeks" is better than "CPL improved significantly." If you don't have a specific number, say so honestly rather than using vague modifiers.

**Conversational-professional**: write like you're explaining something to a smart colleague over coffee. Use contractions. Use first person. Don't write like a textbook, but don't write like a tweet thread either.

**Empathetic**: acknowledge the reader's pain points and constraints. They're busy, they've read a dozen generic guides already.

**Opinionated**: take positions when warranted. "I think X is overrated for B2B, and here's why" is better than "some practitioners have found mixed results with X."

## Voice Attributes We Avoid

**Academic**: no passive voice defaults, no hedging every statement, no "it could be argued that."

**Sales-y**: no "revolutionary," no "game-changing," no "unlock your potential." Sell by being useful, not loud.

**Generic AI**: no "in today's digital landscape," no "comprehensive guide," no "let's dive in." See banned patterns list below.

## Tone Adjustments by Content Type

**How-to guides**: instructional but not condescending. Assume the reader knows the platform basics. Focus on the non-obvious parts.

**Strategy posts**: more opinionated. Share a point of view. It's okay to disagree with common advice if you explain why.

**Data/benchmark posts**: let the numbers lead, but do not make the post emotionally flat. Connect the benchmark to the operator decision it supports: scale, pause, cut spend, defend budget, or explain performance to leadership.

**Tool comparisons**: be fair but decisive. Compare honestly, then give a recommendation with reasoning. Avoid repetitive "X is this, Y is that" definitions.

**Diagnostic posts**: make the pain felt without hype. The reader is usually staring at a dashboard with a problem and needs to know what to check first.

## Formatting Standards

- Short paragraphs: 2–4 sentences max.
- Use bullet lists for features, benefits, or options.
- Use numbered lists for sequential steps.
- Break long content with subheadings every 200–300 words.
- Bold key terms on first use in a section, but don't over-bold.
- One CTA per post linking to your primary CTA page, placed naturally in the flow.
- Cite sources inline when claiming specific numbers, referencing studies, or attributing claims. Link with natural anchor text, not bare URLs or "[source]" links.
- Every post should identify at least one in-article image or video opportunity beyond the OG image. Prefer charts, workflow diagrams, screenshots, annotated examples, or short walkthrough videos.
- If you can't find a credible source for a claim, frame it as opinion: "I've seen..." or "In my experience..."

## Words and Phrases

**Prefer**: use, build, set up, track, test, compare, check, run, pull, fix

**Avoid**: leverage, utilize, unlock, elevate, supercharge, streamline, optimize (unless about actual optimization), empower, goldmine, comprehensive guide, highest-leverage

**Prefer**: "I think," "we've seen," "in my experience"

**Avoid**: "it's widely known that," "experts agree," "studies show" (unless citing a specific study)

## Punctuation and AI-pattern Rules

- Do not use em dashes (U+2014) in blog drafts. Replace them with periods, commas, colons, parentheses, or rewritten sentences.
- Do not use emoji markers in draft copy, callout labels, headings, or summaries.
- Avoid polished contrast structures that repeat across a post, especially in comparison articles.
- Do not default to `Key Takeaways` if a more specific closing section title would feel more natural.
- During final review, search drafts and generated HTML for U+2014 before approving publication.
